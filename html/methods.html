How do we calculate the street O2O index?

  1. Observations

    "As one of many yelp users, searching food online is part of my exploratory life." However, does popular stores among yelp reviewers really contribute to the livelyhood of physical space?
    Inspired by this question, we download review data from 1,500+ stores in Manhattan from yelp, and aggregate these reviews by physical streets. Then we compare the aggregated online popularity with the number of actual visits along these streets, here is what we found:

  2. Model Construction

    To test how much does online reviews actually contribute to physical acitivites, we include other variables in the model.

    * Physical Characteristics: Does Beautiful Space Matters?

    * Business Diversity and Transit: Is Jane Jacobs still right?

    * Local Business: How are local businesses doing?

    * X: We control other variables.

  3. Estimate the Mismatch

    This model was able to achieve around 40% R-square, sigh... Then it means there are a lot of variance could not be explained even after we control the neighborhood variables! Therefore, use this model's residual, we define a O2O mismatch index, which is the residual from the OLS model regressing the total street level visit on online reviews (including the number of reviews and the stars) conditioning on other factors.

  4. Data

    We collect the SafeGraph pattern data* via the SafeGraph COVID-19 Data Consortium and then extract the data from October to December 2019 for our analysis. Our review data are downloaded from Yelp. We use most recent listings in Manhattan areas to estimate the online popularity.

  5. Estimating Street-level Online Popularity and offline Popularity

    All the POI visit data in Manhattan is aggregated towards the nearest street segments using an R-tree algorithm to calculate the total visits of street segments in this 3-month time period. Similarly, the total visits by sectors are calculated following the same manner for street segments.

    Using Yelp review data, we aggregate the number of reviews, average stars of each individual business at street segment level.

  6. Physical Environment Beauty Index

    We use Google Street View images downloaded from 2017 dataset to extract built environment values. This work used a deep learning model created by Fan (2018) With the model and 6,708 street view images in Manhattan, we predict the beauty index of each images, and then aggregate the beauty score by each street segment. The beauty score was normalized.
